# SteadyUs Session Delivery Plan

This document brings the SteadyUs session structure into one practical operating file so Session 1 and Session 2 can be delivered consistently. It is written as an internal playbook rather than customer-facing copy. The aim is to preserve the tone, boundaries, and flow already established across the website content and rebuilt presentation assets.

## Purpose of the offer

The **Couples Budget Reset** is a two-session coaching package designed to help couples who feel capable but financially disorganised, stretched, or stuck. The work is intended to create calmer conversations, clearer priorities, and a shared plan that feels realistic enough to follow in everyday life.

| Core principle | What it means in delivery |
| --- | --- |
| **Calm over pressure** | The sessions should feel steady, non-judgemental, and practical rather than intense or corrective. |
| **Clarity over complexity** | The goal is not technical financial analysis. The goal is a clearer shared picture and better decisions. |
| **Shared ownership** | Both partners should leave knowing what is happening next, rather than one person carrying the mental load alone. |
| **Practical movement** | Every session should end with decisions, priorities, or next steps that can actually be used. |

## Scope boundary

This work is **budgeting support and financial organisation only**. It does **not** include regulated financial, investment, pension, tax, mortgage-product, or legal advice. It is also not debt counselling, therapy, crisis support, or dispute mediation.

This boundary matters during delivery. If a couple asks for specialist recommendations outside the scope above, the session should acknowledge the issue clearly and then redirect back to organisation, priorities, or the next practical decision they can make within the coaching remit.

## Overall delivery rhythm

The package works best when the first session creates visibility and the second session creates movement. The first session should not rush into fixing everything. The second session should not reopen every possible issue. Together, the two sessions should create a simple arc from **understanding** to **agreement** to **action**.

| Session | Main job | Primary outcome |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Session 1** | Make the real money picture visible | Shared clarity about current reality and pressure points |
| **Between sessions** | Keep momentum light and manageable | A few simple prompts, not heavy homework |
| **Session 2** | Turn clarity into practical decisions | A shared action plan with realistic next steps |
| **After the package** | Reinforce follow-through | Written summary and optional follow-up offers |

## Before Session 1

Before the first call, the tone should already feel personal and low-pressure. The couple should know they do not need perfect spreadsheets, perfect behaviour, or fully organised finances in order to start. The job before the session is to reduce friction and help them arrive ready to talk honestly.

| Pre-session aim | Practical approach |
| --- | --- |
| **Lower emotional pressure** | Reassure them that the point is clarity, not judgement. |
| **Set expectations** | Explain that Session 1 is about understanding the current picture, not solving everything immediately. |
| **Reduce preparation burden** | Ask only for light preparation or simple prompts rather than extensive documents. |
| **Encourage joint attendance** | Position the session as shared work, not one partner being assessed. |

## Session 1: Money clarity

Session 1 is designed to slow the conversation down and make the current situation visible. The core objective is to help both partners see the same picture at the same time. This matters because many couples are not stuck through lack of care; they are stuck because the conversation feels loaded, repetitive, or mentally scattered.

The session should move in a calm sequence. First, establish the broad money picture. Then bring hidden pressure points into the open. Finally, identify the main sources of recurring friction. The tone should remain curious and steady throughout. You are not trying to prove a point or assign fault. You are helping the couple understand what is actually happening.

| Session 1 stage | What to do | What good looks like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Open the conversation** | Set a calm tone, explain the session aim, and reinforce that the goal is clarity rather than blame. | Both partners feel they can contribute without being judged. |
| **Map the current money picture** | Bring income, spending, savings, obligations, and current financial pressures into one shared view. | The couple can describe their situation in simple, concrete terms. |
| **Surface pressure points** | Notice where money feels stretched, avoided, emotionally loaded, or repeatedly postponed. | Hidden stress becomes discussable rather than vague. |
| **Identify friction patterns** | Highlight the recurring loops: avoidance, confusion, overload, uneven responsibility, or repeated disagreements. | The couple can name what keeps happening and why it matters. |
| **Close with focus** | Summarise the few issues that matter most and frame what Session 2 needs to solve next. | They leave with clarity, not overwhelm. |

The desired outcome of Session 1 is not a finished plan. It is a **shared starting point**. By the end of the session, both partners should have a clearer sense of what is really going on, what is causing drag, and which topics deserve focused attention next.

### Session 1 facilitator prompts

These prompts are internal cues rather than a script. They should help keep the conversation grounded and useful.

| Prompt type | Example phrasing |
| --- | --- |
| **Clarifying the current picture** | “If we slowed everything down for a moment, what feels most financially unclear right now?” |
| **Finding pressure points** | “Where does money feel heaviest or most emotionally charged at the moment?” |
| **Spotting patterns** | “What conversation do you feel the two of you keep circling back to?” |
| **Reducing blame** | “What feels hard about the system or situation, rather than about either of you as a person?” |
| **Preparing Session 2** | “If next session could make one part of this feel more manageable, what would matter most?” |

## Between sessions

The gap between sessions should keep momentum alive without creating a sense of homework overload. The work here is deliberately light. The couple should not feel that they have failed if they have not built a complete system in the intervening days. Instead, the between-session step should keep the important issues visible and prepare them to make decisions more easily in Session 2.

| Between-session aim | Practical approach |
| --- | --- |
| **Maintain clarity** | Send simple prompts that reflect the main issues identified in Session 1. |
| **Keep the work manageable** | Ask for brief reflection, not a perfect spreadsheet or exhaustive tracking exercise. |
| **Prepare for decisions** | Encourage them to notice what matters most, what feels urgent, and where agreement already exists. |
| **Prevent drift** | Keep the communication light but specific enough that Session 2 starts with momentum. |

## Session 2: Shared action plan

Session 2 should turn understanding into usable decisions. The point is to help the couple decide what matters now, what can wait, and what they will actually do next. If Session 1 makes the situation understandable, Session 2 makes it actionable.

This session should stay selective. The biggest risk is trying to solve every financial issue in one conversation. Instead, narrow the discussion to the few priorities that matter most right now. Then translate those priorities into routines, habits, decisions, and next steps that fit real life.

| Session 2 stage | What to do | What good looks like |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reconnect to Session 1** | Briefly restate the main pressure points and confirm what feels most important now. | Both partners feel that the second session is focused and connected. |
| **Agree realistic priorities** | Identify the small number of financial priorities that deserve attention first. | The couple is no longer trying to fix everything at once. |
| **Turn intentions into habits** | Translate priorities into practical routines, boundaries, or recurring decisions. | The plan feels livable, not idealised. |
| **Define next actions clearly** | Agree who will do what, what will happen next, and what success looks like over the next period. | Both partners leave with clarity rather than assumption. |
| **Close with shared ownership** | Check that the plan feels mutual, realistic, and not carried by one person alone. | The mental load is more evenly held and the next step is explicit. |

The desired outcome of Session 2 is a **shared written plan** with practical next steps. The couple should leave with clearer agreement on priorities, a more manageable way forward, and enough specificity to avoid sliding back into another vague money loop.

### Session 2 facilitator prompts

| Prompt type | Example phrasing |
| --- | --- |
| **Choosing priorities** | “Of everything we surfaced last time, what actually matters most to get clearer on first?” |
| **Making the plan realistic** | “What would feel manageable enough to keep doing even in a busy week?” |
| **Clarifying ownership** | “What needs to be shared here, and what needs one clear owner?” |
| **Preventing future drift** | “What would help stop this from turning back into another vague conversation next month?” |
| **Testing the plan** | “Does this feel like a plan both of you can genuinely follow, not just agree to in theory?” |

## Recommended outputs after the package

After the two sessions, the couple should receive a concise written summary. This should reinforce clarity rather than create a long report. The summary should sound calm and practical, using plain language rather than formal advisory language.

| Output | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| **Priority recap** | Remind the couple what matters most right now. |
| **Agreed next steps** | Record the practical actions they decided on. |
| **Focus points** | Highlight the habits, boundaries, or conversations to keep returning to. |
| **Scope reminder** | Reconfirm that the work is coaching and organisation support, not regulated advice. |
| **Optional follow-up** | Offer the check-in options if helpful for accountability or continuity. |

## Tone guide for delivery

The delivery style should match the website and deck language. It should feel personal, first-person where appropriate, calm, and practical. It should avoid corporate coaching language, fintech jargon, or anything that sounds like regulated financial advice.

| Use more of this | Avoid this |
| --- | --- |
| **Calm, direct language** | Corporate programme language |
| **Practical next steps** | Abstract motivation talk |
| **Shared clarity** | One-sided correction |
| **Real-life manageability** | Idealised perfect-system advice |
| **Gentle structure** | Pressure-heavy transformation claims |

## Best-fit reminder

This package is best suited to couples who are doing reasonably well on paper but feel disorganised, stretched, avoidant, or stuck when it comes to money. It is especially useful where the conversation keeps being postponed, where one partner carries more of the invisible load, or where shared goals exist but the process for getting there feels unclear.

## One-sentence delivery summary

If you want to hold the whole offer in one sentence while delivering it, use this internal summary:

> **Session 1 helps the couple see the real picture together; Session 2 helps them agree what to do next and how to make that plan work in real life.**
